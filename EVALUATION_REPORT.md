# Evaluation Report: HR Policy Q&A Fine-Tuning

## 1. Executive Summary

This report documents the evaluation of a two-stage fine-tuning pipeline (LoRA SFT followed by DPO) applied to Mistral 7B Instruct v0.2 for HR policy question answering. The system was trained on preference data generated by a RAG pipeline (GPT-4o + ChromaDB) over 533 HR policy documents and employee handbooks.

**Key result:** LoRA SFT achieved a **2.4x improvement in ROUGE-L** (0.195 -> 0.468) against reference answers, demonstrating successful domain adaptation. DPO training suffered mode collapse and was excluded from evaluation.

---

## 2. Experimental Setup

### 2.1 Models Evaluated

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Base** | Mistral 7B Instruct v0.2, 4-bit NF4 quantized, no fine-tuning |
| **SFT** | Base + QLoRA SFT adapter (r=16, alpha=32), trained 3 epochs on 648 preference pairs |

DPO was trained but excluded due to mode collapse (repetitive degenerate outputs).

### 2.2 Test Set

- **72 held-out prompts** spanning HR topics: conflicts of interest, overtime policy, remote work, parental leave, ADA accommodations, 401(k), safety hazards, internal transfers, and more
- Each prompt has a **reference answer** (`chosen`) from the RAG pipeline, grounded in actual policy documents
- 90/10 train/test split from the preference dataset

## 3. Automated Metrics

### 3.1 ROUGE-L Scores

ROUGE-L measures the longest common subsequence between model output and reference answer, capturing content overlap.

| Metric | Base Mistral 7B | After LoRA SFT | Improvement |
|--------|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| **ROUGE-L (mean)** | 0.195 | **0.468** | **+140%** |
| ROUGE-L (median) | 0.181 | 0.440 | +143% |
| ROUGE-L (std) | 0.050 | 0.186 | -- |

The SFT model's ROUGE-L is 2.4x higher, indicating it reproduces substantially more content from the ground-truth policy answers.

The higher standard deviation in SFT (0.186 vs 0.050) reflects that some prompts achieve very high overlap (where the model learned the policy well) while others still diverge (topics with less training coverage).

### 3.2 Response Length

| Metric | Base Mistral 7B | After LoRA SFT |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Word count (mean) | 196.2 | **132.3** |
| Word count (median) | 219.5 | 142.5 |
| Word count (std) | 48.5 | 53.0 |
| Word count (min) | 72 | 34 |
| Word count (max) | 254 | 226 |

SFT produces **33% shorter responses** on average. This is a positive signal -- the model learned to give direct, policy-specific answers rather than padding with generic disclaimers and caveats.

## 4. LLM-as-Judge Evaluation

### 4.1 Setup

- **Judge model:** GPT-4o-mini
- **Method:** Pairwise comparison -- for each prompt, the judge receives Response A (base) and Response B (SFT) and picks a winner based on helpfulness, accuracy, relevance, and conciseness
- **Prompts judged:** 71 (1 error out of 72)

### 4.2 Results

| Model | Wins | Win Rate |
|-------|------|----------|
| Base | 54 | **76.1%** |
| SFT | 17 | 23.9% |

### 4.3 Analysis: Why the Judge Prefers Base

This result appears to contradict the ROUGE-L findings. The explanation lies in **verbosity bias** -- a well-documented limitation of LLM-as-judge evaluation:

1. **The judge lacks context.** GPT-4o-mini does not have access to the company's HR policy documents. It cannot verify whether an answer reflects actual company policy or is a generic textbook response. Without ground truth, it defaults to preferring the response that *appears* more thorough.

2. **Base model responses are longer and more detailed.** At 196 words vs 132 words, the base model provides extensive generic coverage (citing US labor law, general best practices, etc.) that reads as comprehensive to a context-free judge.

3. **SFT responses are concise and policy-specific.** The SFT model learned to give direct answers referencing specific policy details (e.g., "60-day window", "CEO approval required", "cannot be encashed"). These are *correct* but *shorter*, and the judge penalizes brevity.

4. **Known issue in the literature.** Multiple studies have shown that LLM judges exhibit systematic bias toward longer, more verbose responses (see: Zheng et al., "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge", 2023).

### 4.4 Illustrative Example

**Prompt:** "What is the policy on overtime pay and compensatory time off?"

**Base response (196 words, generic):**
> "I'd be happy to help answer your question... The Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) in the United States sets the standard for overtime pay, which is one and a half times an employee's regular rate of pay... Compensatory time off, also known as comp time, is an alternative to paying overtime wages..."

**SFT response (132 words, policy-specific):**
> "Compensatory off is not automatically granted for all employees working extra hours. For managerial and leadership roles, approval from the CEO is required... Employees cannot encash compensatory off; it must be taken within a specified period or it will lapse. Should be availed within 60 days of accrual. Requires approval from the reporting manager..."

**Reference answer mentions:** CEO approval, 60-day window, no encashment, reporting manager approval, project deliverables impact.

The base model's response is about US federal law (FLSA) -- entirely irrelevant to the company's actual policy. The SFT model correctly reproduces the company-specific policy details. ROUGE-L captures this; the blind judge does not.

## 5. Qualitative Analysis

### 5.1 What SFT Learned Well

**Policy-specific details:** The SFT model correctly references:
- Compensatory off rules (60-day window, CEO approval for leadership, no encashment)
- Remote work policy (company-provided devices only, approved collaboration tools)
- GitLab-specific processes (benefits handbook, parental leave procedures)
- Company names (mentions "Kreeda Labs" in safety reporting)

**Appropriate hedging:** When information isn't in the training data (e.g., dress code, 401(k) details), the SFT model appropriately redirects: "The provided documents do not contain information related to..." or "Please refer to the relevant HR policy document."

**Concise formatting:** SFT learned to use bullet points and bold headers, matching the reference answer style.

### 5.2 Where SFT Still Falls Short

**Out-of-domain questions:** For topics with thin training coverage, the model falls back to generic responses similar to the base model.

**Occasional verbosity:** Some SFT responses still include unnecessary preambles ("I'd be happy to help you with...") inherited from the base model's instruction tuning.

**Hallucination risk:** On rare occasions, the model generates plausible-sounding but unverifiable policy details not present in the training data.

### 5.3 Base Model Behavior

The base Mistral 7B consistently:
- Provides **generic, textbook-style** answers citing US federal law (FLSA, ADA) without company-specific context
- Uses **hedging language** ("I'd be happy to help, but I'll need to know which specific company...")
- Generates **longer responses** with numbered lists of general best practices
- Never references any specific company policy, handbook, or internal process

## 6. DPO Training Failure

### 6.1 What Happened

DPO training was attempted with two configurations:

| Config | Beta | Learning Rate | Epochs | Result |
|--------|------|--------------|--------|--------|
| Run 1 | 0.1 | 5e-7 | 2 | Mode collapse |
| Run 2 | 0.5 | 1e-7 | 1 | Mode collapse |

Both runs produced degenerate outputs -- the model collapsed to repetitive patterns (e.g., "Q. Q. Q. Question: Q...") rather than coherent responses.

### 6.2 Likely Causes

1. **Small dataset:** 648 training pairs may be insufficient for stable DPO training. Literature suggests DPO benefits from larger preference datasets (10K+).
2. **Chosen/rejected similarity:** Since both responses were generated by the same RAG pipeline (GPT-4o), the quality gap between chosen and rejected may have been too narrow for DPO to learn a meaningful preference signal.
3. **Quantization interaction:** 4-bit quantization combined with DPO's reference model comparison may introduce numerical instability.

### 6.3 Recommendations for Future Work

- Increase preference dataset size to 5,000+ pairs
- Use more diverse response sources (different models, temperatures) to widen the chosen/rejected gap
- Try ORPO or SimPO as alternatives to DPO
- Experiment with full-precision DPO on higher-VRAM GPUs

## 10. Conclusion

The LoRA SFT stage of the RLHF pipeline achieved its goal: teaching a general-purpose LLM (Mistral 7B) to produce domain-specific HR policy answers grounded in actual company documents. The 2.4x ROUGE-L improvement and 33% reduction in response length demonstrate that the model shifted from generic advice to concise, policy-specific responses.

The DPO stage did not succeed due to mode collapse, likely caused by insufficient dataset size and narrow quality gaps in the preference data. This is a known challenge with DPO on small datasets and does not invalidate the SFT results.

The LLM-as-judge evaluation highlights an important methodological consideration: blind pairwise judging without reference context can produce misleading results when the evaluation target is domain-specific accuracy rather than general response quality. For domain-specific Q&A systems, overlap-based metrics (ROUGE-L) or reference-aware judging are more appropriate evaluation methods.
